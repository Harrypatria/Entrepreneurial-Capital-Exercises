Contribution Margin

The foundation of break-even analysis

Click each step to explore

1
Contribution per Unit
▼
Contribution = Selling Price - Variable Cost
This is the amount from each sale that contributes to covering fixed costs and generating profit.
SP: £30
−
VC: £12
=
£18

Every customer visit generates £18 to cover fixed costs and profit.

2
Contribution Margin Ratio
▼
CM Ratio = Contribution per Unit ÷ Selling Price
Expresses contribution as a percentage of sales price. Useful for calculating break-even in revenue terms.
£18
÷
£30
=
0.60 or 60%

For every £1 of sales, 60p contributes to fixed costs and profit.

3
Why It Matters
▼
Higher Contribution = Lower Break-Even Point
Contribution margin is the key to profitability. Strategies to increase it:
  • ✓ Increase selling price - raises contribution directly
  • ✓ Reduce variable costs - negotiate better supplier prices
  • ✓ Add value - justify premium pricing
  • ✓ Volume discounts - reduce per-unit variable costs

Margin of Safety

Your cushion above break-even

1
MOS in Revenue
▼
MOS (£) = Actual Sales - BEP Sales
The absolute amount by which sales exceed break-even.
Actual: £12,000
−
BEP: £10,000
=
£2,000
2
MOS as Percentage
▼
MOS (%) = (MOS £ ÷ Actual Sales) × 100
Expresses the safety margin as a percentage of current sales.
£2,000
÷
£12,000
×
100
=
16.67%
Critical Insight: Sales can drop by 16.67% before the business starts making losses. This is your risk buffer!
3
Interpreting MOS
▼
Risk Assessment Framework
How to evaluate different MOS percentages:
< 10% - High Risk

Very vulnerable to sales fluctuations. Urgent action needed.

10-20% - Moderate Risk

Some cushion but should monitor closely. Room for improvement.

> 20% - Low Risk

Strong safety margin. Business can weather downturns.

EBITDA & EBIT

Understanding operational profitability

EBIT (Operating Profit)

EBIT = Profit Before Tax (PBT) + Interest

Example: RoboClean

PBT: £32,000
+
Interest: £5,000
=
EBIT: £37,000
What it shows: Profit from operations before considering how the business is financed (interest) or taxed. Pure operational performance.

EBITDA Calculation

EBITDA = EBIT + Depreciation + Amortisation

Step-by-Step

EBIT: £37,000
+
Depreciation: £10,000
=
£47,000
£47,000
+
Amortisation: £3,000
=
EBITDA: £50,000
Key Point: EBITDA adds back non-cash expenses (Depreciation & Amortisation) to show cash-based operational performance.
Why is EBITDA preferred for international comparisons?

EBITDA eliminates three major distortions:

1. Financing Differences

Interest rates vary by country and company capital structure. Removing interest allows pure operational comparison.

2. Tax Regime Differences

Corporate tax rates differ globally (US: 21%, UK: 25%, Ireland: 12.5%). EBITDA ignores tax for fair comparison.

3. Accounting Policy Differences

Depreciation methods vary (straight-line vs. accelerated). Removing D&A eliminates this variation.

Scenario 1: The Green Grocer

Break-even and margin of safety analysis

Given Information:
  • Fixed Costs: £6,000/month
  • Selling Price per Visit: £30
  • Variable Cost per Visit: £12
  • Actual Sales: £12,000/month
Q1
Calculate BEP in Sales Revenue (£)
▼
Step 1: Calculate Contribution per Unit
Contribution = £30 - £12 = £18
Step 2: Calculate CM Ratio
CM Ratio = £18 ÷ £30 = 0.60 (60%)
Step 3: Calculate BEP (£)
BEP = £6,000 ÷ 0.60 = £10,000
✓ Answer: £10,000 monthly sales revenue needed to break even
Q2
Calculate BEP in Customer Visits (Units)
▼
Formula: BEP (Units) = Fixed Costs ÷ Contribution per Unit

BEP = £6,000 ÷ £18 = 333.33 customer visits
✓ Answer: 333.33 customer visits per month (round to 334 in practice)
Verification: 333.33 × £30 = £10,000 ✓
Q3
Calculate Margin of Safety (£ and %)
▼
Step 1: MOS in Revenue
MOS (£) = Actual Sales - BEP Sales
MOS (£) = £12,000 - £10,000 = £2,000
Step 2: MOS as Percentage
MOS (%) = (£2,000 ÷ £12,000) × 100
MOS (%) = 16.67%
✓ MOS Revenue: £2,000 | MOS Percentage: 16.67%
Interpretation: The Green Grocer can afford a 16.67% drop in sales (from £12,000 to £10,000) before it starts making losses. This represents moderate risk—there's a cushion, but careful monitoring is advised.

Scenario 2: RoboClean Robotics

EBITDA and EBIT calculations

Given Information:
  • Profit Before Tax (PBT): £32,000
  • Interest Expense: £5,000
  • Depreciation: £10,000
  • Amortisation: £3,000
  • Corporation Tax: £8,000
Q1
Calculate EBITDA
▼
Formula: EBITDA = EBIT + Depreciation + Amortisation

First, we need EBIT:
EBIT = PBT + Interest
EBIT = £32,000 + £5,000 = £37,000
Now calculate EBITDA:
EBITDA = £37,000 + £10,000 + £3,000
EBITDA = £50,000
✓ Answer: EBITDA = £50,000
Q2
Calculate Operating Profit (EBIT)
▼
Formula: EBIT = PBT + Interest

EBIT = £32,000 + £5,000
EBIT = £37,000
✓ Answer: EBIT (Operating Profit) = £37,000
Why add interest back? EBIT shows profit from operations before considering how the business is financed. This allows comparison between companies with different debt levels.
Q3
Why is EBITDA preferred for international comparisons?
▼

EBITDA provides a "clean" measure of operational performance by removing distortions from:

1. Financing Differences (Interest)

Companies have different capital structures (debt vs equity). Removing interest lets you compare operational efficiency regardless of how companies choose to finance themselves.

2. Taxation Regimes (Tax)

Corporate tax rates vary dramatically between countries. EBITDA removes this variable, allowing fair comparison of core business performance.

3. Accounting Policies (D&A)

Different depreciation methods (straight-line, accelerated) and amortisation schedules create artificial differences. EBITDA eliminates these accounting choices to reveal true operational performance.

Bottom Line: When RoboClean competes with a Japanese or German robotics firm, EBITDA allows investors to compare "apples to apples"—focusing purely on how well each company generates profit from its core operations.
