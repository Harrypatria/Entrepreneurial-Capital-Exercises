Account Classification

Understanding the 5 categories of accounts

The 5 Classifications:
CA = Current Asset (convertible to cash within 1 year)
NCA = Non-Current Asset (long-term, used beyond 1 year)
CL = Current Liability (due within 1 year)
NCL = Non-Current Liability (due beyond 1 year)
E = Equity (owner's stake in the business)

Equity: Owner's Investment & Retained Profits

E
Common Stock
Initial owner investment
E
Retained Earnings
Accumulated profits kept in business
Equity Formula:
Equity = Assets - Liabilities

Equity represents the owner's residual claim after all liabilities are paid.

Why This Equation ALWAYS Holds True:

Everything a business owns (Assets) must be financed by either:

  • Owner investment & retained profits (Equity)

Multiple Choice Q&A

Detailed explanations for all questions

Q3
Which item represents an obligation due beyond one year?
▼
A. Accounts Payable
B. Taxes Payable
C. Deferred Tax Liabilities ✓
D. Accounts Receivable
Correct Answer: C - Deferred Tax Liabilities

Deferred tax liabilities represent future tax obligations that extend beyond one year, making them a non-current liability.

Why other options are incorrect:
  • A. Accounts Payable - Current liability (due within 1 year)
  • B. Taxes Payable - Current liability (due soon)
  • D. Accounts Receivable - Asset, not a liability
Q4
What is the fundamental accounting equation?
▼
A. Profit = Revenue - Expenses
B. Assets = Non-Current Assets + Equity
C. Equity = Assets - Liabilities ✓
D. Liabilities = Assets - Equity
Correct Answer: C - Equity = Assets - Liabilities

This is a rearrangement of the fundamental equation: Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Both forms are correct and must always balance.

Why other options are incorrect:
  • A. This is the Income Statement equation, not Balance Sheet
  • B. Incomplete - ignores current assets and liabilities
  • D. Technically correct but less commonly used. Option C is the standard form
Q5
Company issues shares for cash - what's the double-entry?
▼
A. Debit Equity, Credit Cash
B. Debit Cash, Credit Common Stock (Equity) ✓
C. Debit Cash, Credit Accounts Payable
D. Debit Revenue, Credit Cash
Correct Answer: B - Debit Cash, Credit Common Stock
Cash (Asset)
Debit ✓
Cash Increases
Credit
Common Stock (Equity)
Debit
Credit ✓
Equity Increases
Logic: When investors buy shares, cash (asset) increases → debit. Owner's equity increases → credit. Both sides increase equally.
Q6
What happens when a Liability account is Debited?
▼
A. It increases
B. It decreases ✓
C. It remains the same
D. It is transferred to the Income Statement
Correct Answer: B - It decreases
Liability Account
Debit (Decreases)
Paying off debt
Credit (Increases)
Borrowing more
Remember: Liabilities have a natural CREDIT balance. To decrease them, you must DEBIT. This is opposite to assets!
Q7
Which asset is typically the most liquid?
▼
A. Inventory
B. Equipment
C. Cash & Cash Equivalents ✓
D. Accounts Receivable
Correct Answer: C - Cash & Cash Equivalents

Cash is THE definition of liquidity - it's already in liquid form and requires no conversion.

Liquidity Hierarchy (Most → Least Liquid):
  1. Cash - Already liquid
  2. Accounts Receivable - Collected within 30-90 days
  3. Inventory - Must be sold first
  4. Equipment - Long-term asset, hard to convert quickly
Q8
What are accumulated profits kept in the business called?
▼
A. Common Stock
B. Total Assets
C. Retained Earnings ✓
D. Accounts Payable
Correct Answer: C - Retained Earnings
Retained Earnings = Opening RE + Net Income - Dividends
Understanding Retained Earnings:
  • Part of Equity section
  • Profits that were NOT distributed as dividends
  • Reinvested back into the business
  • Increases each year profits are retained
  • Decreases when dividends are paid or losses occur
Why other options are incorrect:
  • A. Common Stock - Initial investment by owners, not accumulated profits
  • B. Total Assets - What the company owns, not retained profits
  • D. Accounts Payable - A liability, not equity
