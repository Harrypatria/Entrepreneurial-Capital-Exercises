Financial Ratios Analysis

Master the four key categories of financial ratios for comprehensive business analysis

💧
Liquidity
Measures a company's ability to meet short-term obligations with liquid assets
  • Current Ratio
  • Quick Ratio (Acid-Test)
Current = CA / CL
Quick = (CA - Inv) / CL
⚖️
Solvency
Evaluates a company's ability to meet long-term debt obligations
  • Debt-to-Equity Ratio
  • Debt-to-Assets Ratio
D/E = Total Debt / Equity
D/A = Total Debt / Assets
⚡
Efficiency
Assesses how effectively a company uses its assets to generate revenue
  • Asset Turnover Ratio
  • Inventory Turnover
Asset TO = Revenue / Assets
💰
Profitability
Measures a company's ability to generate profits from its operations
  • Return on Assets (ROA)
  • Return on Equity (ROE)
ROA = (NI / Assets) × 100
ROE = (NI / Equity) × 100
💡 Key Insight: Financial ratios provide a standardized way to compare companies of different sizes and across different industries. They transform raw numbers into meaningful metrics for decision-making.

Scenario 1: Global Gadgets Inc.

Liquidity Analysis - Current Ratio & Quick Ratio

🏢 Company Overview

Global Gadgets Inc. is planning its inventory levels for the next quarter. Management wants to assess the company's current ability to meet its short-term debt without relying on selling stock.

Account Amount
Cash £15,000
Accounts Receivable £30,000
Inventory £25,000
Accounts Payable £18,000
Short-Term Bank Overdraft £12,000
Long-Term Mortgage £50,000
Important: Current Assets (CA) = £70,000 | Current Liabilities (CL) = £30,000 | Inventory = £25,000
Q1
Calculate Current Ratio
▼
Current Ratio = Current Assets / Current Liabilities

Step 1: Identify Current Assets

✓ Cash: £15,000
✓ Accounts Receivable: £30,000
✓ Inventory: £25,000
Total Current Assets = £70,000

Step 2: Identify Current Liabilities

✓ Accounts Payable: £18,000
✓ Short-Term Bank Overdraft: £12,000
✗ Long-Term Mortgage: NOT included (non-current)
Total Current Liabilities = £30,000

Step 3: Calculate the Ratio

£70,000
÷
£30,000
=
2.33
✓ Answer: Current Ratio = 2.33

This means the company has £2.33 in current assets for every £1 of current liabilities.

Is This Healthy?

Yes! ✓ A current ratio of 2.33 is considered very healthy. Here's why:
  • Benchmark: A ratio of 1.5-3.0 is generally considered good
  • Interpretation: The company can cover its short-term debts 2.33 times over
  • Safety Margin: Even if some assets can't be quickly converted to cash, there's still a comfortable cushion
  • Financial Health: Indicates strong liquidity and low short-term financial risk
Q2
Calculate Quick Ratio (Acid-Test Ratio)
▼
Quick Ratio = (Current Assets - Inventory) / Current Liabilities

Why Exclude Inventory?

⚡ Conservative Measure: The Quick Ratio provides a more stringent test of liquidity by excluding inventory, which may take time to sell and convert to cash. It measures immediate liquidity.

Step 1: Calculate Quick Assets

Inventory: £25,000

Quick Assets Breakdown:

✓ Cash: £15,000
✓ Accounts Receivable: £30,000
✗ Inventory: £25,000 (excluded)
Total Quick Assets = £45,000

Step 2: Calculate the Ratio

£45,000
÷
£30,000
=
1.5
✓ Answer: Quick Ratio = 1.5

The company has £1.50 in liquid assets for every £1 of current liabilities.

Comparison: Current vs Quick Ratio

Metric Current Ratio Quick Ratio
Value 2.33 1.5
Includes Inventory ✓ Yes ✗ No
Liquidity Test General Stricter
Interpretation Overall healthy Strong immediate liquidity
📊 Key Insight:
  • Quick Ratio of 1.0 or higher is generally considered good
  • At 1.5, Global Gadgets can meet all short-term obligations without selling inventory
  • The difference (2.33 - 1.5 = 0.83) represents the inventory cushion
  • Both ratios indicate excellent short-term financial health

Scenario 2: BioPharma Labs

Solvency & Profitability Analysis

🧬 Company Overview

BioPharma Labs, a startup in a capital-intensive industry, reports the following year-end balances. A new investor is interested in the company's debt structure and asset utilization.

Account Amount
Total Liabilities £150,000
Total Equity £100,000
Net Income £20,000
Total Assets £250,000
Q1
Calculate Debt-to-Equity Ratio
▼
Debt-to-Equity Ratio = Total Liabilities / Total Equity

What Does This Ratio Measure?

The Debt-to-Equity ratio shows the proportion of debt financing relative to equity financing. It indicates how much the company relies on borrowed funds versus owner investment.

Calculation:

£150,000
÷
£100,000
=
1.5
✓ Answer: Debt-to-Equity Ratio = 1.5

For every £1 of equity, the company has £1.50 in debt.

What Does a Ratio > 1 Mean?

⚠️ Interpretation: A ratio greater than 1 typically indicates:
  • Higher Leverage: The company uses more debt than equity for financing
  • Increased Risk: Greater financial risk due to debt obligations
  • Industry Context: In capital-intensive industries (like biotech), ratios above 1 are common and acceptable
  • Growth Stage: Startups often have higher ratios while building their business
  • Interest Obligations: More debt means more interest payments, affecting profitability

Q2
Calculate Return on Assets (ROA)
▼
ROA = (Net Income / Total Assets) × 100

What Does ROA Tell Investors?

Return on Assets (ROA) measures how efficiently a company uses its assets to generate profit. It shows how much profit is earned for each pound invested in assets.

Calculation:

£20,000
÷
£250,000
×
100
=
8.0%
✓ Answer: Return on Assets = 8.0%

The company generates £0.08 in profit for every £1 of assets.

Investor Insights:

What This 8.0% ROA Tells the Investor:
  • Asset Efficiency: Management is generating reasonable returns from the company's assets
  • Profitability: For every £100 in assets, the company makes £8 in profit
  • Benchmark: 5% or higher is generally considered good; 8% is solid
  • Investment Returns: Helps compare with alternative investment opportunities
  • Management Quality: Indicates effective use of resources
💡 Context Matters: ROA should be compared to:
  • Industry averages (capital-intensive industries typically have lower ROA)
  • Company's historical performance
  • Competitors in the same sector
  • Cost of borrowing (interest rates)
Q3
Calculate Return on Equity (ROE)
▼
ROE = (Net Income / Total Equity) × 100

What Does ROE Measure?

Return on Equity (ROE) measures the return generated on shareholders' investment. It's one of the most important metrics for equity investors as it shows profitability from the shareholders' perspective.

Calculation:

£100,000

✓ Answer: Return on Equity = 20.0%

Shareholders earn £0.20 for every £1 invested in equity.

ROA vs ROE Comparison:

Metric ROA ROE
Value 8.0% 20.0%
Denominator Total Assets Total Equity
Perspective All capital (debt + equity) Shareholders only
Interpretation Asset efficiency Shareholder returns
🔍 Why is ROE Higher than ROA?

ROE (20%) is significantly higher than ROA (8%) because of financial leverage. The company uses debt financing, which magnifies returns to equity holders. This is the effect of the 1.5 debt-to-equity ratio.

Overall Assessment for BioPharma Labs:

  • Strong ROE (20%): Excellent returns for shareholders
  • Solid ROA (8%): Good asset utilization
  • Moderate Leverage (1.5): Reasonable for the industry, but requires monitoring
  • Profitable Operations: £20,000 net income demonstrates viability
  • Investment Verdict: Attractive for growth-oriented investors who can accept moderate risk
